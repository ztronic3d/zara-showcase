📖 My Story

At 12 years old, I discovered 3D printing and it changed everything. What started with one printer in my room making a few toys evolved into something extraordinary—four printers and an entire wearable, working Iron Man suit.

Building this suit taught me far more than just printing. I learned how printers really work, how to fix them when they break, electronics and wiring, painting techniques for realistic details, and countless life lessons about persistence, problem-solving, and creativity.

My school recognized my passion and gave me access to their makerspace—an entire room full of broken printers to fix. After restoring them, I brought other students into the fold. In 7th grade, we launched our own school store through ITeam, creating and selling products that generate around $1,000 in revenue each year. That group continues to grow, teaching more students every semester.

As a young woman of color in STEM, representation matters deeply to me. I know what it's like to walk into a room and not see anyone who looks like you. That's exactly why I share my story—to show young girls, especially girls of color, that they belong in engineering, technology, and maker spaces. If I can build a full Iron Man suit and a robotic arm by teaching myself through curiosity and persistence, so can they. I want every young girl to know that her ideas are valid, her creativity is powerful, and there's a place for her in STEM.

🦾 Latest Project Just Completed

3D Printed Robotic Arm Mechanism — My newest creation is a fully functional robotic arm built from 3D printed components, controlled by an Arduino microcontroller and servo motors.

What makes this project special? I taught myself everything through YouTube tutorials and AI assistance. This demonstrates exactly what I talk about in my presentations: with curiosity, persistence, and the resources available today, students can learn almost anything.

The arm features precise servo-controlled movements, custom-designed joints for smooth articulation, and demonstrates the intersection of mechanical engineering, electronics, and programming—all skills I developed outside the traditional classroom.
